    Planning and Development

    The processing of planning applications, building control, and local spatial planning.

    About this area

    Planning and development sits in the Business Areas layer of the Local Government Architecture Model. It covers the statutory functions that councils perform around land use, building safety, and spatial planning - from processing individual planning applications through to maintaining the local plan.

    This page demonstrates a framework for assessing your local authority's planning capabilities, identifying fragmentation, and building a modern, data-driven roadmap for your Planning and Development functions using the LGAM taxonomy.

    2. Map your capabilities and technology

    Under each core LGAM capability, record the applications or digital services your department currently uses. This structured mapping ensures every capability has a consistent trail from domain to enabling technology, allowing you to identify duplication (e.g. using multiple different form builders). The below is a non-exhaustive example of how you might approach this:

    3. Assess capability maturity and risk

    Evaluate how well your mapped capabilities work together. Are you relying on manual data entry? Are your forms disconnected from your workflow? Use a heat map to visually communicate capability strength, interoperability, and risk to stakeholders.

    The cost of fragmentation

    Analysis from the Reducing Invalid Planning Applications (RIPA) and Back Office Planning System (BOPS) Local Digital Fund business cases demonstrates the high cost of legacy practices and the significant potential savings of resolving capability gaps.

    50% Average invalid rate

    If your Forms capability lacks built-in logic and is disconnected from your GIS, you will see high invalidation rates (historically ~50%). Resolving these manual errors can take 2-5 hours per application.

    80% Validation improvement

    Using user-centred, logic-driven forms that integrate deeply with your capability stack can prevent up to 80% of common validation errors at the point of entry.

    £66k+ Annual LA savings

    Estimated minimum processing time savings for a typical local authority (processing ~1,400 applications/year) by reducing the invalid rate. Savings could reach £171,500.

    0.5 Days saved by applicants

    Estimated time saved by an applicant or agent when preparing an application using modernised tools, plus avoiding a further half-day of subsequent corrections.

    4. Build an actionable roadmap

    Translate your assessment insights into a capability-driven improvement plan. Extensive public sector user research has established the following evidence-based principles as rationalisation targets for planning technology.

    Strategic rationalisation targets

    Moving from "documents to data"

    Prioritise collecting standardised, machine-readable data via modern forms. Reduce reliance on your Document Management capability for unstructured PDFs to power automated checking.

    Modular architecture

    Retire monolithic systems that tightly couple your Forms, Workflow, and Payments. Transition toward API-driven, modular services to prevent vendor lock-in and improve agility.

    User-centred design

    Redesign back-office workflows around the verified needs of planning officers, automating routine validation checks through improved interoperability.

    5. Interoperability target: Planning Data Platform

    The Planning Data Platform is a central repository built by the Ministry of Housing, Communities and Local Govenment that standardises planning and housing data across England. It serves as the national interoperability target for your local capabilities.

    Local authorities should set long-term ambitions to align their Data and Geographical capabilities with MHCLG's specifications. By maintaining spatial data in standard formats at the source, councils can automate the flow of data via APIs, removing the need for manual reporting.
