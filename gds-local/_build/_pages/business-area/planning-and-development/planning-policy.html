      Planning Policy

      The technical architecture required to gather spatial evidence, engage citizens at scale, and formulate the statutory Local Plan.

      Creating a Local Plan is a multi-year, data-intensive endeavor. Unlike the transactional nature of Development Control, Planning Policy architecture focuses on big data analytics, geospatial modeling (e.g., finding land suitable for housing), and mass citizen engagement platforms designed to capture sentiment rather than just simple form inputs.

      The LGAM technology stack

      Delivering a Local Plan requires orchestrating analytical and engagement capabilities.

      LGAM Taxonomy Node Domain Specific Implementation
      Geospatial data Spatial modelling to conduct Multi-Criteria Decision Analysis (MCDA), overlaying flood zones, transport nodes, and conservation areas to identify viable development sites.
      Data warehousing Aggregating ONS demographic data, historic housing delivery rates, and economic models into a central repository to form the "evidence base".
      Websites (Engagement) Interactive, map-based consultation platforms allowing citizens to drop pins and leave sentiment-driven feedback on proposed zoning changes (Regulation 18/19).
      API management Once the Plan is adopted, exposing the spatial policies as "Rules-as-code" APIs to be consumed directly by the Development Control forms engines.

      The technical lifecycle

      This section outlines the process of building the Local Plan. Expand each phase to compare the legacy architectural baseline against modern, data-driven target states.

      1 Evidence Base & Site Allocation

      • Business Process
      • Legacy Anti-patterns
      • Target Architecture

      The process: Policy officers must gather massive amounts of demographic, environmental, and economic data. They model this data to predict future housing needs and identify physical plots of land suitable for development (the Strategic Housing Land Availability Assessment - SHLAA).

      Required LGAM Taxonomy Nodes:

      Geospatial data Data warehousing Business intelligence
      Anti-pattern: Manual desktop spatial clicks

      Conducting complex site allocation through undocumented, manual operations within desktop GIS software. The analyst manually intersects layers (e.g., "Remove flood zones, add brownfield"). If the underlying flood data updates next week, the entire manual clicking process must be repeated, and errors are untraceable.

      Best Practice: Reproducible Analytical Pipelines (RAP)

      Target architectures treat the Local Plan evidence base as code. Data scientists use languages like Python/R or enterprise ETL tools to create documented, version-controlled pipelines. These scripts automatically pull live data from the Corporate Data Lake and OS APIs, execute the spatial math (Multi-Criteria Decision Analysis), and output the results dynamically.

      Real-world impact
      • Legal Defensibility

        When developers challenge the Local Plan at Examination, the council can demonstrably prove the exact mathematical weighting and data lineage used to allocate a specific site, removing human bias.

      • Iterative Efficiency

        If the housing target changes by 5%, the policy team simply updates one variable in the script and reruns the entire spatial model in minutes, rather than spending weeks manually re-drawing maps.

      2 Public Engagement & Consultation

      • Business Process
      • Legacy Anti-patterns
      • Target Architecture

      The process: The council must consult the public on the draft plan. Citizens, developers, and statutory bodies review proposed zoning changes and provide feedback, generating thousands of responses that must be logged, categorized, and addressed.

      Required LGAM Taxonomy Nodes:

      Websites (Consultation) Data pipelines
      Anti-pattern: The PDF and Inbox

      Publishing the draft Local Plan as a massive, 300-page static PDF on the council website and asking citizens to email their feedback to a generic inbox. This creates a data-sorting nightmare for officers, who must manually read, categorize, and copy-paste thousands of unstructured emails into a spreadsheet.

      Best Practice: Geospatial Engagement Platforms

      Modern planning policy uses dedicated engagement platforms (e.g., Commonplace, Citizen Space). The plan is presented as an interactive web map. Citizens click on specific zones to leave feedback. The platform immediately forces the feedback into structured JSON, attaching exact geographic coordinates and categorizing sentiment before the data ever reaches the policy team.

      Real-world impact
      • Democratic Reach

        Significantly increases public participation by lowering the barrier to entry (from reading a 300-page PDF to interacting with a mobile-friendly map).

      • Administrative Savings

        Eliminates hundreds of hours of manual administrative sorting. Sentiment dashboards allow officers to instantly see the primary areas of contention across the borough.

      3 Adoption & Integration

      • Business Process
      • Legacy Anti-patterns
      • Target Architecture

      The process: Following Examination by the Planning Inspectorate, the Local Plan is officially adopted. This plan is now the legal framework that Development Control officers must use to assess incoming planning applications.

      Required LGAM Taxonomy Nodes:

      API management Event-driven integration
      Anti-pattern: The Disconnected Truth

      The Planning Policy team publishes the new rules as a PDF document. Meanwhile, Development Control continues to use older spatial layers in their separate IT system. There is no automated connection between the newly adopted rules and the systems used to enforce them daily.

      Best Practice: Rules-as-Code API Publishing

      Modern architectures align with the MHCLG objective of "Rules-as-code". When a Local Plan is adopted, its spatial constraints (e.g., Conservation Areas, Tree Preservation zones) are published to the corporate data store and instantly exposed via an API. The Development Control "dynamic forms" frontend is hardwired to consume this API, meaning applicants immediately face the new rules the moment the plan is legally adopted.

      Real-world impact
      • Service Cohesion

        Closes the critical gap between Policy creation and Dev Control enforcement. It ensures the council acts as a single entity using a single source of truth.

      • National Alignment

        By exposing these policies as structured APIs, the council automatically complies with central government mandates to publish local plan data to planning.data.gov.uk.

      Target data models

      Planning Policy is the primary generator of spatial rules. These outputs must precisely align with MHCLG national schemas so they can be universally understood by the market.

      Dataset Schema Architectural Purpose
      Local plans The overarching schema for the spatial development strategy. It must be published as structured data, not just text, defining exact geographic boundaries.
      Brownfield land Statutory register of previously developed land. Maintaining this as a live, API-accessible schema rather than an annual CSV export ensures continuous compliance.

      Platforms & tools

      Because Planning Policy spans deep analytics and broad public engagement, the tooling usually involves a mix of Enterprise GIS and specialized web platforms.

      • Esri ArcGIS Hub: Extends traditional spatial analysis into community engagement, allowing councils to build map-centric consultation websites.
      • Commonplace: A dedicated, map-based community engagement platform focused on capturing structured, location-specific citizen sentiment.
      • Citizen Space (Delib): A widely used government consultation platform for managing statutory consultation responses efficiently.
      • Cloud Data Warehouses (Snowflake, AWS, Azure): Used increasingly by data-mature councils to run reproducible spatial models (MCDA) rather than relying on desktop GIS software.
